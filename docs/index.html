What you can do

Pick a site

Start from any of the 46 NEON terrestrial sites across 23 states — from Arctic tundra to Sonoran desert grassland to agricultural Midwest — and the whole app retunes to that community in an instant.

Cover composition

The Overview reads each site as native vs introduced percent cover plus its ground-cover makeup — litter, bare soil, rock, wood — so you see the structure of the community, not just a species list.

Diversity, done properly

Walk the nested 1→400 m² species-area curve, read a Hill-number profile that scales from richness to evenness, and get a Chao2 incidence estimate of the species the plots haven’t caught yet.

Native vs invasive lens

Track the introduced-cover trend over time, scan a per-site invasive watchlist, and read the 1 m² invasion-pressure foothold index — how often non-natives are gaining a toe-hold at the smallest scale.

Environment — the new headline

The star tab: does diversity actually track annual climate & phenology? Every correlation ships with a permutation null and a matched-n, so on a short record it tells you “not enough years to say” instead of inventing a trend.

Diversity Lab — tap to pin

Every plot is a tap-to-pin dot. Pin the ones you care about into persistent profile cards, then open a downloadable Plot Profile card for any one — the per-plot record, ready to share.

Statistics

Every view leads with transparent, well-sourced methods:

  • Cover is an index, not a census — NEON percent cover is an ocular, relative estimate, and vegetation layers overlap, so a plot’s cover can sum past 100%. It’s read as relative composition, never as an exact area.
  • Diversity — Hill numbers give one continuous family from richness through evenness; richness is extended with Chao2, an incidence-based estimator over the nested subplots, reported with its confidence interval rather than a bare count.
  • Environment correlation — climate & phenology relationships are tested against a permutation null on a matched-n sample, so a site with only a handful of survey years is told it has too short a record to claim a trend, not handed a spurious one.
  • Unknown nativity is published — some taxa can’t be confidently called native or introduced, so the unknown-nativity rate is shown alongside every native-vs-introduced split instead of being silently folded into one side.
